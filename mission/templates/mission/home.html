Physical Explanation

This simulator models the trajectory of Pioneer 11 in three successive phases, each governed by a distinct two-body problem: (1) a geocentric hyperbola at departure, (2) a heliocentric ellipse from Earth to Jupiter, and (3) a heliocentric hyperbola from Jupiter to Neptune, unlocked by a gravitational assist at Jupiter. The following sections derive every formula used in the simulation from first principles. Throughout this report, $\mu = GM$ denotes the gravitational parameter of the central body, and all quantities are expressed in the International Astronomical System (km, km/s, seconds).

1 — Departure Hyperbola (Geocentric Phase)

1.1 Parking orbit and injection burn

The probe begins on a circular parking orbit of radius $r = R_E + z_p$, where $R_E = 6\,371\ \text{km}$ is Earth's mean radius and $z_p$ is the parking altitude. On a circular orbit, gravitational acceleration exactly provides the centripetal force:

$$\frac{\mu_E}{r^2} = \frac{V_c^2}{r} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad V_c = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_E}{r}}$$ with $\mu_E = 398\,600\ \text{km}^3\text{/s}^2$.

An impulsive burn (Hohmann-type $\Delta V$ at perigee) raises the speed to $V_a$. The required $\Delta V$ is simply: $$\Delta V = V_a - V_c$$

1.2 Specific orbital energy and characteristic energy $C_3$

The specific mechanical energy (energy per unit mass) of the resulting trajectory is given by the vis-viva equation, which combines kinetic and potential energy:

$$\mathcal{E} = \frac{V^2}{2} - \frac{\mu_E}{r} = \text{const along the trajectory}$$ Evaluated at the burn point $r$, with $V = V_a$: $$\mathcal{E} = \frac{V_a^2}{2} - \frac{\mu_E}{r}$$

The sign of $\mathcal{E}$ determines the conic section: $\mathcal{E} < 0$ → ellipse (bound orbit), $\mathcal{E} = 0$ → parabola (escape with zero residual speed), $\mathcal{E} > 0$ → hyperbola (escape with positive residual speed). The characteristic energy is defined as $C_3 = 2\mathcal{E}$, which is the standard launch performance metric in mission design: $$C_3 = V_a^2 - \frac{2\mu_E}{r}$$

1.3 Hyperbolic excess velocity $V_\infty$

At infinite distance from Earth ($r \to \infty$), the potential energy $-\mu_E/r \to 0$, so all the specific energy is kinetic. The vis-viva equation gives:

$$\mathcal{E} = \frac{V_\infty^2}{2} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad V_\infty = \sqrt{2\mathcal{E}} = \sqrt{C_3}$$

$V_\infty$ is the hyperbolic excess velocity: the speed the probe retains after escaping Earth's gravity well. It is the speed that the probe carries into interplanetary space and is the key quantity for designing the heliocentric transfer.

1.4 Hyperbola geometry and asymptote angle

For a hyperbolic orbit, the semi-major axis $a_h$ is defined as negative (convention for unbound orbits): $$a_h = -\frac{\mu_E}{2\mathcal{E}} = -\frac{\mu_E}{C_3} < 0$$ The eccentricity is related to the perigee radius $r$ and the semi-major axis by: $$e_h = 1 - \frac{r}{a_h} = 1 + \frac{r\,C_3}{\mu_E} > 1$$ The asymptote of the hyperbola makes an angle $\varphi^*$ with the apse line (the line connecting Earth to the perigee). This angle satisfies $\cos\varphi^* = -1/e_h$, derived from the polar equation of the conic at $r \to \infty$:

$$r = \frac{a_h(e_h^2-1)}{1 + e_h\cos\nu} \xrightarrow{r\to\infty} 1 + e_h\cos\nu_\infty = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \cos\varphi^* = -\frac{1}{e_h}$$

The angle $\varphi^*$ sets the departure direction in the geocentric frame and must be aligned with the direction toward Jupiter in the heliocentric frame.

1.5 Time to exit the Sphere of Influence

Earth's Sphere of Influence (SOI) is approximated by the Hill sphere radius: $$r_{\rm SOI} \approx r_T \left(\frac{m_E}{3\,m_S}\right)^{1/3} \approx 924\,000\ \text{km}$$ The time for the probe to travel from the burn point to the SOI boundary is computed using the hyperbolic analogue of Kepler's equation. Introducing the hyperbolic anomaly $F$, defined by: $$r = a_h(1 - e_h\cosh F)$$ and solving for $F$ at $r = r_{\rm SOI}$, the time of flight is:

$$t_1 = \sqrt{\frac{(-a_h)^3}{\mu_E}}\,(e_h\sinh F_{\rm SOI} - F_{\rm SOI})$$
Physical interpretation: $t_1$ is typically a few days. During this time the probe is still dominated by Earth's gravity. Beyond the SOI, the Sun becomes the dominant body and the geocentric description is abandoned in favour of the heliocentric one.

2 — Heliocentric Injection

2.1 Earth's orbital velocity

Earth orbits the Sun on a nearly circular orbit at radius $r_T = 149\,597\,870\ \text{km}$ (1 AU). Its orbital velocity is: $$V_T = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_S}{r_T}} \approx 29.78\ \text{km/s} \qquad \mu_S = 1.327\times10^{11}\ \text{km}^3\text{/s}^2$$

2.2 Velocity composition at the SOI boundary

At the SOI boundary, the probe's velocity in the geocentric frame has magnitude $V_\infty$ and is directed along the outgoing asymptote. In the heliocentric frame, this velocity must be added vectorially to Earth's orbital velocity $\vec{V}_T$. Applying the law of cosines to the velocity triangle:

$$V_P = |\vec{V}_T + \vec{V}_\infty| = \sqrt{V_T^2 + V_\infty^2 + 2\,V_T V_\infty\cos\theta}$$ where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{V}_T$ and $\vec{V}_\infty$. Defining $\alpha = \pi - \theta$ (supplement angle for convenience): $$V_P = \sqrt{V_T^2 + V_\infty^2 - 2\,V_T V_\infty\cos\alpha}$$

The angle $\alpha$ is determined by the departure geometry. To maximise $V_P$ (and thus the heliocentric energy), one ideally sets $\alpha = 0$ (prograde injection, $\vec{V}_\infty$ parallel to $\vec{V}_T$), giving $V_{P,\max} = V_T + V_\infty$. In practice, $\alpha$ is constrained by the required Jupiter encounter geometry.

2.3 Flight path angle $\beta$

The flight path angle $\beta$ is the angle between the heliocentric velocity vector $\vec{V}_P$ and the local horizontal (tangent to Earth's orbit). It is given by: $$\tan\beta = \frac{V_\infty\sin\alpha}{V_T - V_\infty\cos\alpha}$$ A non-zero $\beta$ means the probe is not departing along a Hohmann transfer and the transfer orbit perihelion does not coincide exactly with Earth's orbit radius.

3 — Earth → Jupiter Transfer (Heliocentric Ellipse)

3.1 Semi-major axis from energy

In the heliocentric frame, the probe's specific energy at departure is: $$\mathcal{E}_{\rm helio} = \frac{V_P^2}{2} - \frac{\mu_S}{r_T}$$ For a bound transfer to Jupiter ($\mathcal{E}_{\rm helio} < 0$), the vis-viva relation gives the semi-major axis:

$$a_2 = -\frac{\mu_S}{2\,\mathcal{E}_{\rm helio}} = \frac{\mu_S}{2\mu_S/r_T - V_P^2}$$

3.2 Eccentricity

The eccentricity $e_2$ of the transfer ellipse is found from the perihelion condition. The perihelion radius of the ellipse is the point where the probe moves purely tangentially, i.e., the radial velocity is zero. Using the orbit equation: $$r_{\rm peri} = a_2(1 - e_2) \qquad r_{\rm aph} = a_2(1 + e_2)$$ If we assume the departure is at (or near) perihelion at $r_T$, and the arrival is near aphelion at $r_J$:

$$e_2 = \frac{r_J - r_T}{r_J + r_T}$$ (This is the classical Hohmann transfer eccentricity. When $\beta \neq 0$, $e_2$ is computed from the full angular momentum vector instead.)

3.3 Time of flight via Kepler's equation

The time of flight on an elliptic arc cannot be expressed in closed form using the true anomaly $\nu$. Kepler introduced the eccentric anomaly $E$ via the geometric relation: $$r = a_2\,(1 - e_2\cos E)$$ The eccentric anomaly at Earth's orbit (perihelion, $r = r_T$) is $E_0 = 0$. The eccentric anomaly $\varphi_2$ at Jupiter's orbit ($r = r_J$) is found by solving:

$$r_J = a_2\,(1 - e_2\cos\varphi_2) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \cos\varphi_2 = \frac{1 - r_J/a_2}{e_2}$$

The mean anomaly $M$ is defined to grow linearly with time: $M = n\,t$ where $n = \sqrt{\mu_S/a_2^3}$ is the mean motion. Kepler's equation relates $M$ to $E$: $$M = E - e_2\sin E$$ The time elapsed between perihelion ($E = 0$) and the eccentric anomaly $\varphi_2$ is therefore:

$$t_2 = \frac{1}{n}\,(\varphi_2 - e_2\sin\varphi_2) = \sqrt{\frac{a_2^3}{\mu_S}}\,(\varphi_2 - e_2\sin\varphi_2)$$
For a classical Hohmann transfer to Jupiter, $t_2 \approx 2.74\ \text{years}$. Pioneer 11's actual flight time to Jupiter was about 1.9 years due to a more energetic injection ($V_P > V_{\rm Hohmann}$).

4 — Jupiter Arrival

4.1 Probe speed at Jupiter via vis-viva

Energy is conserved along the heliocentric transfer ellipse. The specific energy computed at departure equals that at arrival: $$\frac{V_{S1}^2}{2} - \frac{\mu_S}{r_J} = \frac{V_P^2}{2} - \frac{\mu_S}{r_T} = \mathcal{E}_{\rm helio}$$ Solving for $V_{S1}$:

$$V_{S1} = \sqrt{V_P^2 + 2\mu_S\!\left(\frac{1}{r_J} - \frac{1}{r_T}\right)}$$

4.2 Jupiter's orbital velocity and relative speed

Jupiter moves on a nearly circular orbit at $r_J \approx 778.5 \times 10^6\ \text{km}$: $$V_J = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_S}{r_J}} \approx 13.07\ \text{km/s}$$ The probe's velocity relative to Jupiter (expressed as a vector difference) is: $$\vec{V}_R = \vec{V}_{S1} - \vec{V}_J$$ Its magnitude $V_R = |\vec{V}_R|$ and its direction (angle $\gamma$ relative to the tangential direction) fully characterise the entry conditions into the Jovian hyperbola. Using the law of cosines:

$$V_R = \sqrt{V_{S1}^2 + V_J^2 - 2\,V_{S1}\,V_J\cos\gamma}$$

This $V_R$ plays the role of $V_\infty$ for the Jovian two-body problem: it is the speed the probe has relative to Jupiter when it is far from the planet.

5 — Jupiter Swing-by (Jovian Hyperbola)

5.1 Geometry of the Jovian hyperbola

In the Jupicocentric frame, the probe enters with speed $V_R$ at large distance. The closest approach (perijove) is set at altitude $z_J$ above Jupiter's cloud tops ($R_J = 71\,492\ \text{km}$): $$r_{pJ} = R_J + z_J$$ At perijove, all specific energy is conserved (vis-viva), giving:

$$\frac{V_{pJ}^2}{2} - \frac{\mu_J}{r_{pJ}} = \frac{V_R^2}{2} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad V_{pJ} = \sqrt{V_R^2 + \frac{2\mu_J}{r_{pJ}}}$$ with $\mu_J = 1.267 \times 10^8\ \text{km}^3\text{/s}^2$.

5.2 Eccentricity of the Jovian hyperbola

For a hyperbolic orbit, the perijove radius is related to the semi-major axis by $r_{pJ} = |a_J|(e_h - 1)$ (the probe passes the focus on the near side). The semi-major axis of the Jovian hyperbola is $|a_J| = \mu_J / V_R^2$ (derived from $\mathcal{E}_J = V_R^2/2 = \mu_J/(2|a_J|)$). Substituting:

$$e_h - 1 = \frac{r_{pJ}}{|a_J|} = \frac{r_{pJ}\,V_R^2}{\mu_J} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad e_h = 1 + \frac{r_{pJ}\,V_R^2}{\mu_J}$$

Since $r_{pJ} > 0$ and $V_R > 0$, we always have $e_h > 1$, confirming the hyperbolic nature. A larger perijove $r_{pJ}$ or a larger relative speed $V_R$ both increase $e_h$, which reduces the deflection (the hyperbola becomes more "open").

5.3 Deflection angle

The probe enters along the inbound asymptote and exits along the outbound asymptote, both passing through the focus (Jupiter). The true anomaly of the asymptote $\nu_\infty$ satisfies: $$1 + e_h\cos\nu_\infty = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \nu_\infty = \arccos\!\left(-\frac{1}{e_h}\right) \in \left(\frac{\pi}{2},\pi\right)$$ The total deflection angle $\delta$ between the inbound and outbound velocity vectors is:

$$\delta = 2\nu_\infty - \pi$$ Using $\sin(\delta/2) = \sin(\nu_\infty - \pi/2) = -\cos\nu_\infty = 1/e_h$: $$\boxed{\delta = 2\arcsin\!\left(\frac{1}{e_h}\right)}$$
The deflection $\delta$ is maximised when $e_h \to 1$ ($\delta \to \pi$, a U-turn) and decreases as $e_h$ increases. For Pioneer 11, $\delta \approx 70°$ to $90°$ depending on the perijove altitude.

5.4 Energy gain mechanism

In the Jupicocentric frame, the field is conservative: the magnitude $|\vec{V}_R|$ is strictly conserved throughout the flyby (only the direction rotates by $\delta$). However, in the heliocentric frame, the outgoing Jupicocentric velocity $\vec{V}_R'$ (rotated by $\delta$) composes differently with $\vec{V}_J$:

$$\vec{V}_{S2} = \vec{V}_J + \vec{V}_R' \qquad \text{(after the flyby)}$$ $$|\vec{V}_{S2}|^2 = V_J^2 + V_R^2 + 2\,V_J\,V_R\cos\theta_{\rm out}$$ compared to the entry: $|\vec{V}_{S1}|^2 = V_J^2 + V_R^2 + 2\,V_J\,V_R\cos\theta_{\rm in}$

The energy gain is $\Delta\mathcal{E} = (V_{S2}^2 - V_{S1}^2)/2 = V_J V_R(\cos\theta_{\rm out} - \cos\theta_{\rm in})$. By choosing a trailing-side flyby (the probe passes behind Jupiter in its orbital direction), the rotation $\delta$ turns $\vec{V}_R$ to be more aligned with $\vec{V}_J$, so $\cos\theta_{\rm out} > \cos\theta_{\rm in}$, and $\Delta\mathcal{E} > 0$. The probe gains energy; Jupiter loses the same amount — but given Jupiter's mass ($1.9\times10^{27}\ \text{kg}$), the effect on its orbit is entirely negligible.

6 — Swing-by Exit and Heliocentric Escape

6.1 Post-flyby heliocentric velocity

After the deflection by angle $\delta$, the outgoing Jupicocentric velocity $\vec{V}_R'$ has the same magnitude $V_R$ but its direction has rotated by $\delta$ relative to the inbound direction. In the heliocentric frame: $$\vec{V}_{S2} = \vec{V}_J + \vec{V}_R'$$ The exit angle $\alpha_3$ (angle of $\vec{V}_{S2}$ with the Sun–Jupiter radial axis) is found by projecting both components onto the radial and tangential axes.

6.2 Post-flyby heliocentric energy and escape condition

The specific heliocentric energy after the swing-by, evaluated at Jupiter's position $r_J$, is:

$$E_2 = \frac{V_{S2}^2}{2} - \frac{\mu_S}{r_J}$$

The heliocentric escape condition at distance $r_J$ from the Sun requires: $$V_{S2} \geq V_{\rm esc,\odot}(r_J) = \sqrt{\frac{2\mu_S}{r_J}} \approx 18.5\ \text{km/s}$$ Equivalently, $E_2 > 0$. When this condition is met, the probe is on a hyperbolic heliocentric trajectory and will escape the Solar System without any additional thrust.

Pioneer 11 achieved $E_2 > 0$ after its Jupiter flyby in December 1974, becoming one of the first man-made objects on a Solar System escape trajectory, alongside Pioneer 10.

7 — Jupiter → Neptune Transfer (Hyperbolic Solar Trajectory)

7.1 Equations of motion and energy integral

On the outbound trajectory, the probe is subject only to the Sun's gravity (all planetary perturbations neglected). The specific mechanical energy is conserved: $$\mathcal{E} = \frac{V^2}{2} - \frac{\mu_S}{r} = E_2 = \text{const} > 0$$ The velocity can be decomposed into radial $\dot{r}$ and tangential $r\dot{\nu}$ components. Using the conservation of specific angular momentum $L = r^2\dot{\nu}$:

$$V^2 = \dot{r}^2 + \frac{L^2}{r^2} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \dot{r} = \frac{dr}{dt} = \sqrt{2\!\left(E_2 + \frac{\mu_S}{r}\right) - \frac{L^2}{r^2}}$$

7.2 Approximation and flight time integral

Beyond Jupiter, the probe's trajectory is nearly radial: the angular momentum term $L^2/r^2$ decreases as $1/r^2$ and becomes negligible compared to the energy term $2E_2$ for large $r$. This approximation simplifies the integral significantly:

$$\frac{L^2}{r^2} \ll 2E_2 \quad\text{for } r \gg r_J \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \dot{r} \approx \sqrt{2E_2 + \frac{2\mu_S}{r}}$$ Separating variables: $$dt = \frac{dr}{\sqrt{2E_2 + 2\mu_S/r}}$$ Integrating from Jupiter ($r_J$) to Neptune ($r_N$): $$t_3 = \int_{r_J}^{r_N} \frac{dr}{\sqrt{2\!\left(E_2 + \dfrac{\mu_S}{r}\right)}}$$

This integral has no closed-form solution in general and is evaluated numerically. Neptune is at $r_N \approx 4.495\times10^9\ \text{km} \approx 30.07\ \text{AU}$.

Pioneer 11 reached Neptune's orbit in 1990, approximately 16 years after launch. At that distance, the probe was travelling at roughly 10 km/s and radio signals took about 4 hours to reach Earth.

The table below tracks the specific heliocentric energy $\mathcal{E}$ at each mission phase. The gravitational assist is the only mechanism that increases the heliocentric energy without propellant expenditure:

Jupiter loses exactly the same amount of energy, but given its mass ($M_J \approx 1.9\times10^{27}\ \text{kg}$), the corresponding change in its orbital speed is less than $10^{-20}\ \text{m/s}$ — truly negligible.
