🧠 Detailed Physical Explanation

🌍 1. Earth Escape Phase

The spacecraft starts in a circular orbit around Earth at altitude \( z_p \). The distance from Earth's center is:

$$ r = R_E + z_p $$

The circular velocity is obtained from gravitational equilibrium:

$$ V_{200} = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_E}{r}} $$

A propulsion burn adds \( \Delta V \), giving:

$$ V_p = V_{200} + \Delta V $$

If this velocity exceeds escape velocity, the spacecraft enters a hyperbolic trajectory. The orbit energy becomes positive:

$$ \epsilon = \frac{V_p^2}{2} - \frac{\mu_E}{r} > 0 $$

From this, we derive the semi-major axis (negative for hyperbola):

$$ a = \frac{\mu_E}{V_p^2 - \frac{2\mu_E}{r}} $$

The hyperbolic excess velocity represents the velocity far away from Earth:

$$ V_{\infty} = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_E}{a}} $$

This is the key quantity used for interplanetary transfers.

Escape time is computed using hyperbolic anomaly:

$$ t = \sqrt{\frac{a^3}{\mu_E}} (e \sinh E - E) $$

---

☀️ 2. Heliocentric Phase

Once the spacecraft leaves Earth's sphere of influence, we switch to a frame centered on the Sun.

Earth moves at:

$$ V_E = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_S}{r_{ES}}} $$

The spacecraft velocity relative to the Sun is obtained by vector addition:

$$ V_{S0} = \sqrt{V_E^2 + V_{\infty}^2 + 2 V_E V_{\infty} \cos(\theta)} $$

This comes directly from the law of cosines.

The angle \( \theta \) determines whether the spacecraft gains or loses energy.

---

🛰️ 3. Transfer Orbit

The spacecraft now follows an orbit around the Sun. The total energy is:

$$ \epsilon = \frac{V^2}{2} - \frac{\mu_S}{r} $$

From this energy, we compute the semi-major axis:

$$ a = -\frac{\mu_S}{2\epsilon} $$

This defines the shape of the transfer orbit.

At Jupiter distance, velocity becomes:

$$ V = \sqrt{2\left(\frac{\mu_S}{r} - \frac{\mu_S}{2a}\right)} $$

Jupiter itself moves at:

$$ V_J = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_S}{r_J}} $$

The relative velocity between spacecraft and Jupiter is:

$$ V_{\infty,J} = \sqrt{V^2 + V_J^2 - 2 V V_J \cos(\gamma)} $$

This determines how strong the gravity assist will be.

---

🪐 4. Jupiter Flyby

Near Jupiter, the spacecraft follows a hyperbolic trajectory:

$$ a_H = \frac{\mu_J}{V_{\infty,J}^2} $$

The eccentricity is:

$$ e = 1 + \frac{r_p}{a_H} $$

The turning angle of the trajectory is:

$$ \varphi = \arccos\left(\frac{1}{e}\right) $$

This angle determines how much the spacecraft trajectory is deflected.

The gravity assist modifies the spacecraft velocity direction, allowing:

  • Increase of heliocentric energy 🚀
  • Change of trajectory direction
  • Access to outer planets
